# Setting Up Your Pantry

This page brings together advice from two great resources: Lorna Sass' *Complete
Vegetarian Kitchen* (originally *Recipes from an Ecological Kitchen*, which features
a 70-page A-to-Z glossary on selecting and storing vegetarian pantry ingredients),
and Stephanie McKercher's *The College Vegetarian Cookbook* (whose "Cooking 101"
chapter covers kitchen setup and must-have staples for beginners). Here's our take
on a well-stocked pantry.

## The Foundation

McKercher emphasizes keeping versatile, non-perishable staples on hand so you can
whip up a meal without relying on takeout. Her core list: dry or canned beans,
lentils, pasta, rice, oats, nut butter, frozen vegetables, and affordable proteins
like tofu. From these, endless combinations are possible.

Sass goes broader, organizing her pantry around whole grains (wheat, corn, rice,
barley, quinoa), legumes (beans, lentils, dried peas), aromatics (onions, garlic,
ginger), and a well-curated spice collection. Her glossary also covers selection
— how to tell if grain is fresh, how to store flours, what to look for when buying
dried beans.

## Our Pantry Categories

### Grains & Starches

White and brown rice, rolled oats, pasta, and rice noodles. These are the quick
fillers — add lentils or beans and you've got a meal.

### Legumes

Keep both dried and canned. Canned for speed (drain, rinse, use), dried for
economy and better texture. Red lentils cook fastest (~15 min), brown and green
lentils take 20-30, chickpeas and dried beans need soaking or a pressure cooker.

### Flavor Builders

- Onions, garlic, ginger. A jar of minced garlic is a miracle of the modern
  kitchen — always keep one in the fridge. Same for ginger (even a tube of
  ginger paste works in a pinch)
- Cumin, turmeric, smoked paprika, chilli powder, black pepper, salt — the
  spice rack that turns plain lentils into something you'd look forward to
- Soy sauce / tamari, Better Than Bouillon, hot sauce, vinegars
- Canned tomatoes, tomato paste

### Fats

Olive oil for sautéing and dressing, vegetable oil for high-heat cooking, toasted
sesame oil for finishing. These keep well and make food taste like food.

### Quick Proteins

Extra-firm tofu, eggs (if you eat them), nut butters. These round out a meal when
you haven't planned ahead.

## Keep It Real

You don't need every ingredient in Sass' glossary or McKercher's shopping list to
start. Buy what you'll actually use in a week, then add one or two new staples
each shop. Soon enough you'll open the cupboard and see a meal before you even
look at a recipe.

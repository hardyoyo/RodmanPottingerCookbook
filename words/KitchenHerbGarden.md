# Kitchen Herb Garden

You can buy pots of basil and cilantro at the grocery store, and they're handy —
but they were raised in a greenhouse with ideal light, humidity, and heat. Packed
tightly into small pots (Mark Ridsdill Smith notes a typical supermarket basil pot
holds about 50 seedlings in just 3 inches of soil), they're designed for short-term
use, not long-term survival on your windowsill.

If you want a real kitchen herb garden, plant from seed.

## The Basics

**Pots.** Use ceramic or stoneware pots (black or dark colors help absorb warmth).
Each pot needs a drainage hole and a drip tray or saucer, or you'll ruin your
windowsill. Aim for 4-6 inch pots for individual herbs.

**Soil.** Standard potting mix works fine. Make sure it's loose and drains well.

**Light.** Most culinary herbs need 4-6 hours of direct sunlight daily. A
south-facing window is ideal. Rotate pots regularly so they don't lean toward
the light.

**Water.** Keep soil moist but not waterlogged. Water when the top inch feels dry.
Pots without drainage will kill your plants.

## Basil

The workhorse of the kitchen garden. Start from seed indoors 6-8 weeks before the
last frost, or sow directly in a pot anytime indoors. Basil needs warmth — soil
should be at least 70°F for germination. Pinch off the tips regularly to encourage
bushy growth (and use the pinchings in your cooking). If summer comes, move basil
outside to a sunny, sheltered spot — it'll be much happier out there. Genovese is
the classic variety, but Thai basil, lemon basil, and dark opal basil are all easy
to grow from seed and fun to have on hand.

Source: Mark Ridsdill Smith, *Vertical Veg* (substack.com); The Old Farmer's Almanac
(almanac.com/plant/basil).

## Cilantro (Coriander)

Cilantro prefers cooler conditions than basil — 60-70°F is ideal. It has a long
taproot and doesn't like being transplanted, so always start from seed directly
in the pot you'll keep it in. Soak seeds for 12-24 hours before planting to
improve germination. Sow every 3 weeks for a continuous supply. Look for
slow-bolting varieties like Calypso or Leisure, which stay productive longer.
Once it flowers (bolts), the leaves lose flavor — but let some go to seed, and
you'll have coriander for the pantry.

Source: Grow Organic (groworganic.com); Stephanie McKercher, *The College
Vegetarian Cookbook*.

## Others (Just for Fun)

Basil and cilantro are the big two — the ones you'll use most weeks. Other herbs
are worth growing if you have a recipe that calls for them, or if you just want
to experiment:

- **Mint** — grows like crazy. Keep it in its own pot or it'll take over. Great
  for tea and garnishes.
- **Chives** — low maintenance, perennial, and pretty purple flowers. Snip into
  soups and salads. But if you plant them outside they *will* take over the
  garden — they're hooligans.
- **Oregano** — prefers drier soil and lots of light. Good dried or fresh on
  pizza and in tomato sauces.
- **Thyme** — hardy and forgiving. Adds depth to soups, stews, and marinades.
- **Parsley** — notoriously boring, but indispensable for tabouleh, falafel, and
  all sorts of other salads. Easy to grow, needs a bit more water than the
  others. Flat-leaf (Italian) is more flavorful than curly.
- **Rosemary** — tricky indoors, but worth trying if you have very bright light
  and good airflow.

## Moving Outside

If you're coming up on summer, be prepared to move your baby plants outside.
Most culinary herbs grow better outdoors with real sun and airflow. Acclimate
them gradually — a few hours a day for a week — before leaving them out full
time. Bring them back in before the first frost.
